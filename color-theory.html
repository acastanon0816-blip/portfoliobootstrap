What is Color Theory?

Color theory determines how colors work together and how they impact perception and meaning. In design, color theory helps designers select color combinations to communicate a message, create mood, and improve readability and user experience.

Key Color Theory Terms

Hue
The basic “name” of a color, such as red, blue, or green.
Saturation
The intensity of a color (high saturation is vivid; low saturation is muted).
Value
Scale of lightness or darkness of a color.
Tint
A lighter version of a color when mixed with white.
Shade
A darker version of a color formed by mixing it with black.
Tone
A color mixed with gray, reducing intensity without only lightening or darkening.
Primary Colors
Colors (commonly red, yellow, and blue in RYB) that when combined create other colors.
Secondary Colors
Colors made by mixing two primary colors (e.g., green, orange, purple in RYB).
Tertiary Colors
Colors made by mixing a primary and a secondary color (e.g., red-orange).
Complementary Colors
Colors opposite each other on the color wheel.
Analogous Colors
Colors that sit next to each other on the color wheel.
Triadic Scheme
A color scheme that employs three colors evenly spaced around the color wheel.
Split-Complementary Scheme
A base color plus the two colors adjacent to its complement.
Monochromatic Scheme
A color scheme that uses tints, tones, and shades of one hue.
Warm Colors
Colors associated with warmth (reds, oranges, yellows).
Cool Colors
Colors associated with coolness (blues, greens, purples).
Color Harmony
Pleasing combinations of colors that feel balanced and intentional.
Contrast
The difference between elements (often light vs dark) that improves readability and emphasis.
Color Temperature
Whether a color feels warm or cool and how it affects mood.
Accessibility (Color Contrast)
Designing color choices so text and important elements are readable for all users.
